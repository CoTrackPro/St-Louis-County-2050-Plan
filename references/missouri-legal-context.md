# Missouri Legal Context — Laws Affecting County Government Operations

in St. Louis County. Understanding these laws prevents giving generic
advice that doesn't apply in Missouri.

**Disclaimer**: This is educational reference material, not legal advice.
Consult County Counselor or qualified attorney for specific legal questions.

## 1. Sunshine Law

Missouri's Sunshine Law (RSMo Chapter 610) governs public access to
government records and meetings. It is one of the broadest open records
laws in the country.

### Key Provisions

- **Records presumed open**: All records of a public governmental body
  are presumed open unless specifically closed by statute (§610.011).
- **Meetings presumed open**: All meetings, records, votes, actions, and
  deliberations of a public governmental body are presumed open (§610.010).
- **Closed records exceptions**: Personnel records, individually identifiable
  personnel records, legal communications, security measures, and certain
  other categories may be closed (§610.021).
- **Response timeline**: Public body must respond to records requests within
  3 business days (end of 3rd business day). Can take additional time if
  needed but must notify requester.
- **Fees**: Can charge actual cost of search, copying, and staff time for
  retrieval. Cannot charge for review or redaction.

### KPI Program Implications

| What's Open | What's Closed | Gray Area |
|------------|--------------|-----------|
| Department KPI scores and trends | Individual employee performance reviews | Employee assessment scores (aggregate = open; individual = likely closed) |
| Council briefings and scorecards | Individual KSAB proficiency ratings | Training completion data (aggregate = open; individual = ask counsel) |
| Budget and expenditure data | Personnel investigation files | Vendor performance scores on contracts |
| Meeting minutes and agendas | Draft working documents (limited exemption) | Internal improvement plans (generally open once finalized) |
| Constituent survey results (aggregate) | Individual complaint details with PII | De-identified complaint patterns |

**Best practice**: Design the KPI system assuming everything *except*
individual personnel data will be Sunshine-requested. Build reports with
this transparency in mind — no surprises.

**Common Sunshine Request scenario**: A reporter or council member requests
"all KPI data for the Police Department for the past 12 months." If the
data exists, you must provide it. Build data systems with exportability
in mind.

## 2. Hancock Amendment

The Hancock Amendment (Mo. Const. Art. X, §§ 16–24, passed 1980) limits
tax increases and new government activities funded by existing taxes.

### Key Provisions

- **Tax levy ceiling**: Political subdivisions cannot increase tax rates
  or add new taxes without voter approval.
- **Revenue growth limit**: State and local government revenue growth is
  tied to personal income growth. Revenue above the limit must be refunded.
- **New activity funding**: State cannot mandate new activities for local
  government without providing funding.

### KPI Program Implications

- **Fee increases** for permits, licenses, or services may trigger Hancock
  Amendment scrutiny if they exceed cost of providing the service. Revenue
  KPI targets must be set within legal fee authority.
- **New programs** recommended by KPI analysis (new digital portal, new
  service center) require identifying existing budget capacity — cannot
  simply raise taxes to fund them.
- **Shared services** with municipalities may have Hancock implications
  if the arrangement effectively creates a new taxing mechanism.
- **Efficiency savings** from KPI improvements cannot be characterized as
  "revenue increases" — they are cost avoidance, not new revenue.

## 3. County Government Structure

St. Louis County operates under a charter form of government (first-class
county).

### Key Structural Features

- **County Executive**: Elected, serves as chief executive. Appoints
  department heads. 4-year term.
- **County Council**: 7 members, each representing a district. Legislative
  body. Passes ordinances, approves budget. 4-year terms, staggered.
- **Separation of powers**: Executive and legislative branches are distinct.
  Council cannot direct department operations; Executive cannot pass laws.
- **Charter amendments**: Require voter approval.

### Implications for KPI Programs

- **Executive branch** can implement KPI programs through executive action
  for departments under their authority.
- **Council approval needed** for: budget allocations, new ordinances,
  organizational restructuring, new positions.
- **Council oversight** includes requesting reports, holding hearings, and
  approving the annual budget — all opportunities for KPI data to be used
  (positively or negatively).
- **Charter officers** (Prosecuting Attorney, Sheriff in some MO counties)
  operate independently and may resist executive-branch KPI programs.

## 4. Civil Service & Merit System

St. Louis County operates a civil service system for most non-appointed
positions.

### Key Features

- **Merit-based hiring**: Positions filled through competitive examination
  and certification process, not at-will.
- **Classified vs. unclassified**: Most positions are classified (civil
  service protections). Department heads and certain senior positions are
  unclassified (serve at pleasure of appointing authority).
- **Step pay system**: Compensation follows a step/grade schedule.
  Performance-based pay adjustments are limited.
- **Disciplinary process**: Progressive discipline with procedural due
  process requirements. Cannot terminate classified employees without
  cause and process.
- **Promotion**: May involve competitive examination. Seniority is often
  a factor alongside merit.

### KPI Program Implications

| What You CAN Do | What You CANNOT Do |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Track department-level KPIs | Use KPI data to bypass disciplinary process |
| Use KSAB assessments for training needs | Use KSAB scores as sole basis for termination |
| Identify knowledge/skill gaps for professional development | Create mandatory "performance improvement" tied to KPI scores without following civil service procedures |
| Set department-level targets that inform budget requests | Change job descriptions or pay grades without classification review |
| Recognize high-performing teams | Create informal bonus or incentive structures outside the pay plan |

**Critical rule**: The IDP template is a *development* tool, not a
disciplinary tool. Performance improvement plans that could lead to
discipline must follow civil service procedures — different process,
different documentation, different legal footing.

## 5. Labor Relations

### Missouri Context

- Missouri is a **right-to-work** state (RSMo §290.590). Employees cannot
  be required to join a union as a condition of employment.
- **Public sector collective bargaining**: Missouri does not have a
  comprehensive public sector collective bargaining statute like some
  states. However, St. Louis County has historically recognized and
  negotiated with employee unions.
- **Meet and confer**: County may have a "meet and confer" obligation
  with recognized unions on working conditions.

### KPI Program Implications

- **Notify union before implementing** any system that could be perceived
  as monitoring individual employee performance.
- **Frame as department-level measurement**, not individual surveillance.
- **Cross-training** assignments may affect job classifications — check
  union agreement before rotating staff between departments.
- **Schedule changes** (e.g., extending hours at one location, adding
  Saturday service) may require union consultation.
- **Training requirements** — adding new mandatory training may be a
  negotiable change in working conditions.

## 6. POST Commission

The Missouri Peace Officer Standards and Training (POST) Commission
(RSMo §590) governs law enforcement training requirements.

### Key Requirements

- **Basic training**: All peace officers must complete POST-certified
  basic training (academy).
- **Continuing education**: Licensed peace officers must complete
  continuing education hours per licensing cycle.
- **Specific mandates**: Certain training topics are mandated by statute
  or POST rules:
  - Racial profiling awareness
  - Use of force
  - De-escalation (increasingly required)
  - Pursuit driving
  - Legal updates
- **License revocation**: POST can revoke peace officer license for
  cause, including failure to meet training requirements.

### KPI Program Implications

- Police training KPIs must align with POST requirements — POST mandates
  are non-negotiable minimums, not targets.
- KSAB competency maps for police (PD-K1 through PD-B2) should reference
  POST-mandated topics where applicable.
- Training hours for police officers are partially dictated by POST
  — county training budget must account for these fixed requirements
  before discretionary professional development.
- Body camera policies, use-of-force reporting, and community engagement
  metrics should align with POST guidance and any pending legislation.

## 7. Public Records Retention

Missouri Secretary of State establishes records retention schedules
for local government (RSMo §109).

### Relevant Retention Periods

| Record Type | Minimum Retention | Notes |
|------------|-------------------|-------|
| Financial records | 5 years after audit | Budget, expenditures, revenue |
| Personnel records | Duration of employment + 3 years | Individual files |
| Meeting minutes | Permanent | Council and committee |
| Correspondence | 3 years | General business |
| Training records | Duration of employment + 3 years | Completion, certifications |
| Complaints/grievances | 3 years after resolution | |
| Contracts | 6 years after expiration | |

### KPI Program Implications

- **KPI measurement data** is likely a "report" — retain per financial
  records schedule (5+ years). This is actually good for trend analysis.
- **KSAB assessment data** is personnel-adjacent — retain per personnel
  records schedule. Clarify with Records Management.
- **Training enrollment records** — retain per training records schedule.
- **Council briefings** — retain permanently as part of council records.
- **Don't delete unfavorable data** — Sunshine Law and records retention
  apply regardless of whether the numbers are good or bad.

## 8. Procurement — Chapter 107

St. Louis County procurement is governed by Chapter 107 of the County Code.

### Key Requirements

- **Competitive bidding**: Required for purchases above threshold
  (typically $10,000–$25,000 depending on category).
- **Sealed bids**: Formal sealed bid process for larger contracts.
- **S/M/WBE goals**: County has participation goals for small, minority,
  and women-owned business enterprises.
- **Sole source**: Allowed with justification and approval, but
  discouraged and documented.
- **Professional services**: May use qualifications-based selection
  rather than low bid.
- **Emergency procurement**: Expedited process available for genuine
  emergencies with documented justification.

### KPI Program Implications

- **Technology purchases** (KPI dashboard software, survey tools, data
  systems) must go through procurement. Plan 60–120 days for procurement
  cycle in implementation timeline.
- **Training vendors** — external training providers are procured services.
  Sole-source for specialized training may be justified but needs documentation.
- **Procurement KPIs** (cycle time, bid response) are measuring the
  procurement process itself — circular but necessary.
- **S/M/WBE participation** is both a KPI to track and a procurement
  requirement to follow when buying KPI program infrastructure.

## 9. Budget & Finance Requirements

### Budget Cycle

- **Fiscal year**: Typically January 1 – December 31 for Missouri counties
  (verify for STL County — some use July 1 – June 30).
- **Budget submission**: County Executive submits proposed budget to
  Council.
- **Council review**: Public hearings, amendments, and adoption.
- **Appropriation**: Council appropriates funds by department/category.
- **Budget amendments**: Mid-year changes require Council approval.

### Financial Reporting

- **Annual audit**: Required by state law. Conducted by independent auditor.
- **GFOA standards**: County may seek GFOA Distinguished Budget
  Presentation Award (structured budget with performance data).
- **Federal grants reporting**: CDBG, HUD, FEMA require specific
  financial reporting tied to program performance.

### KPI Program Budget Impact

- KPI programs should be funded through existing operating budget where
  possible (analyst time, IT systems already in place).
- New technology investments (dashboard platform, data warehouse) require
  capital budget allocation or grant funding.
- Training budget increases recommended by TNA require Council approval
  in annual budget cycle.
- Frame KPI program ROI in terms of: avoided costs (reduced rework,
  lower complaint volume), revenue optimization (fee collection), and
  grant compliance (avoiding HUD findings).

## 10. Open Meetings

### Missouri Open Meetings Law (RSMo §610.010–610.035)

- All meetings of public governmental bodies must be open to the public
  unless a specific statutory exception applies.
- **Agenda posting**: Required in advance (24 hours for regular, reasonable
  notice for special meetings).
- **Closed session**: Permitted for specific topics (personnel, litigation,
  real estate, security) with recorded vote to close and documentation of
  topics discussed.
- **Voting**: Must occur in open session. No secret ballots.

### KPI Program Implications

- **Stat meetings** (internal performance review meetings) between
  department heads and the County Executive are likely not subject to
  open meetings law if they're administrative (not a "governmental body"
  meeting). However, verify with County Counselor.
- **Council committee meetings** that discuss KPI reports are open.
  Prepare for public and media attendance when KPI data is presented.
- **Public performance hearings** — if Council holds a hearing on
  department performance, all KPI data presented becomes fully public record.

## 11. Implications for KPI Programs — Quick Reference

| Legal Constraint | Impact on KPI Program | Mitigation |
|-----------------|----------------------|------------|
| Sunshine Law | All KPI data is presumptively public | Design for transparency from day one. No hidden dashboards. |
| Hancock Amendment | Can't raise taxes/fees to fund improvements | Fund through existing budget or grants. Focus on efficiency, not revenue. |
| Civil Service | Can't use KPIs for at-will discipline | Track department-level metrics. Use KSAB for development, not punishment. |
| Union Relations | Measurement perceived as surveillance | Brief union early. Frame as professional development. Track teams, not individuals. |
| POST Commission | Police training requirements are mandated | Align police KSAB to POST requirements. Budget for mandated training first. |
| Records Retention | Must retain KPI data per schedule | Good for trend analysis. Don't delete bad numbers. |
| Procurement (Ch. 107) | Technology purchases take 60–120 days | Plan procurement timeline into Phase 0. Use existing tools first. |
| Budget Cycle | New spending requires Council approval | Time training budget requests to annual budget cycle. Show ROI data. |
| Open Meetings | Council discussions of KPIs are public | Prepare talking points. Provide context with every metric. |

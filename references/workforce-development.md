## 1. KSAB Framework Overview

Every government role requires four domains of competency. Gaps in any
domain produce different failure modes — and require different interventions.

| Domain | Definition | Failure Mode When Missing | Intervention Type |
|--------|-----------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| **Knowledge** | Facts, concepts, regulations, policies, and domain information an employee must know | Employee doesn't know *what* to do or *why* | Training, documentation, mentoring, job aids |
| **Skills** | Practiced abilities to apply knowledge — technical, interpersonal, analytical, digital | Employee knows what to do but *can't execute* | Hands-on practice, simulations, coaching, on-the-job training |
| **Attitudes** | Beliefs, values, motivations, and dispositions toward work, constituents, and colleagues | Employee *can* do the work but *doesn't want to* or *doesn't see the point* | Leadership modeling, culture initiatives, recognition, engagement programs, role clarity |
| **Behaviors** | Observable, consistent actions that demonstrate competency in practice | Employee has knowledge/skills/attitudes but *doesn't consistently act on them* | Performance management, accountability systems, peer norms, process redesign, feedback loops |

### Diagnostic Principle

When a KPI underperforms, always ask in sequence:
1. **Is it a Knowledge gap?** — Do staff know the standard, the process, the regulation?
2. **Is it a Skill gap?** — Can they execute when tested in a controlled setting?
3. **Is it an Attitude gap?** — Do they believe the work matters? Are they motivated?
4. **Is it a Behavior gap?** — Do they do it consistently in real conditions, under pressure, without supervision?

Training alone fixes only Knowledge and (partially) Skill gaps. Attitude
and Behavior gaps require systemic interventions — leadership, culture,
process design, and accountability.

### Proficiency Levels

| Level | Label | Description |
|-------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | **Foundational** | Can perform with guidance and supervision; knows basics |
| 2 | **Developing** | Can perform independently in routine situations |
| 3 | **Proficient** | Can perform independently in complex situations; can troubleshoot |
| 4 | **Advanced** | Can mentor others; can adapt approach to novel situations |
| 5 | **Expert** | Can design systems, set standards, lead organizational change |

## 4. KSAB Gap Analysis Methodology

### Step 1: Define the Performance Problem
Start with a KPI that's below target. Avoid starting with "we need training"
— start with "what outcome is underperforming?"

### Step 2: Map KPI to Required Competencies
Use the KPI-to-Competency Linkage table (Section 5) to identify which
competencies drive the underperforming KPI.

### Step 3: Assess Current Proficiency
For each linked competency, assess actual proficiency using multiple methods:

| Assessment Method | Best For | Validity | Cost |
|-------------------|----------|----------|------|
| Knowledge test (written/online) | K domain | High for K | Low |
| Skills demonstration (observed task) | S domain | High for S | Medium |
| 360° feedback survey | A and B domains | Medium-High | Medium |
| Supervisor observation checklist | B domain | High for B | Low |
| Mystery shopper / constituent survey | B domain (customer-facing) | High for B | Medium |
| Performance data / system logs | B domain | High (objective) | Low |
| Self-assessment | All domains | Low-Medium (bias) | Low |
| Structured interview | K and A domains | Medium | Medium |

**Best practice**: Use at least two methods for each domain. Never rely
solely on self-assessment.

### Step 4: Calculate Gap Scores

For each competency:
```
Gap Score = Target Proficiency Level − Current Proficiency Level
```

| Gap Score | Priority | Action |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| 0 or negative | None | Competency met; consider advanced development |
| +1 | Low | Reinforcement, refresher, coaching |
| +2 | Medium | Structured training with practice |
| +3 | High | Intensive training + supervised practice + follow-up |
| +4 | Critical | May indicate hiring/role fit issue; training + mentoring + reassessment |

### Step 5: Diagnose Domain

For the same KPI problem, gaps may exist in different KSAB domains for
different employees. The intervention must match the domain:

| If the gap is in... | Don't do this | Do this instead |
|---------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Knowledge** | Assume they'll learn on the job | Formal training, job aids, documentation, mentoring |
| **Skills** | Classroom lecture only | Hands-on practice, simulation, shadowing, coached repetition |
| **Attitudes** | Mandatory training (creates resentment) | Leadership modeling, peer stories, role clarity, connect work to impact, recognition |
| **Behaviors** | More training (they already know how) | Process redesign, checklists, accountability systems, feedback, peer norms, remove barriers |

### Step 6: Design Intervention (see Section 6)

### Step 7: Measure Impact
- **Level 1 — Reaction**: Did participants find the training useful? (post-training survey)
- **Level 2 — Learning**: Did knowledge/skill increase? (pre/post test, skills demo)
- **Level 3 — Behavior**: Did on-the-job behavior change? (observation, system data, 30/60/90 day)
- **Level 4 — Results**: Did the KPI improve? (compare pre/post training period data)

This is Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Model. Most government training programs
measure only Level 1. For KPI-linked development, measure through Level 4.

## 6. Training Modality Guide

Match the modality to the gap domain and the learner context.

### Knowledge Gap Interventions

| Modality | Duration | Best When | Limitations |
|----------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| eLearning modules (self-paced) | 30–60 min | Policy updates, compliance, regulations | No practice, low retention without reinforcement |
| Instructor-led classroom | 2–8 hours | Complex knowledge, discussion-heavy, group cohorts | Scheduling, cost, pulls staff off front line |
| Job aids (quick reference cards, checklists) | N/A (always available) | Procedural knowledge, rare tasks, new systems | Doesn't build deep understanding |
| Mentoring / knowledge transfer | Ongoing | Institutional knowledge, judgment, context | Depends on mentor quality, time-intensive |
| Documentation / SOPs | N/A | Authoritative source of truth | Only works if regularly updated and accessible |
| Lunch-and-learn / brown bag | 30–60 min | Awareness, new initiatives, cross-department sharing | Low depth, optional attendance |

### Skill Gap Interventions

| Modality | Duration | Best When | Limitations |
|----------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| On-the-job training (OJT) | Days–weeks | Practical skills, systems, workflows | Quality depends on trainer, may slow production |
| Simulation / role play | 1–4 hours | De-escalation, customer service, inspections | Requires facilitation, can feel artificial |
| Shadowing | 1–5 days | Complex roles, cross-training | Passive without structured observation guide |
| Coached practice with feedback | Ongoing | Any skill requiring refinement | Requires skilled coach, time commitment |
| Certification programs | Weeks–months | Technical specialties (assessor, inspector, planner) | Cost, time away from work |
| Cross-training rotations | 2–4 weeks | Multi-skill development, succession planning | Temporary productivity dip |

### Attitude Gap Interventions

| Modality | Duration | Best When | Limitations |
|----------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Leadership modeling and messaging | Ongoing | Culture shift, new values adoption | Slow; requires genuine leadership buy-in |
| Peer stories / testimonials | 30–60 min | Connecting work to impact, purpose | Can feel forced if not authentic |
| Site visits to high-performing peers | 1–2 days | Showing what's possible, inspiration | Cost, scheduling |
| Recognition programs | Ongoing | Reinforcing desired attitudes | Can backfire if perceived as manipulation |
| Role clarity conversations | 1 hour | When staff don't see how their work matters | Requires good supervisor facilitation |
| Constituent feedback sharing | 15 min (recurring) | Connecting staff to impact of their work | Must balance positive and constructive |
| Employee engagement survey + action | Annual + follow-up | Diagnosing systemic attitude issues | Only works if leadership acts on results |

### Behavior Gap Interventions

| Modality | Duration | Best When | Limitations |
|----------|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Process redesign (make right behavior the easy behavior) | Weeks | Systemic behavior issues | Requires process authority, change management |
| Checklists and protocols | N/A | Ensuring consistent execution of known procedures | Can become rote / checked-without-doing |
| Performance dashboards (visible to team) | Ongoing | Creating peer accountability and awareness | Can create unhealthy competition if poorly managed |
| Regular 1:1 feedback | 15–30 min weekly | Individual behavior coaching | Requires trained supervisors |
| Peer observation | 30–60 min | Calibrating behavior standards across team | Requires psychological safety |
| Mystery shopper / audit | Per event | Objective behavior measurement (customer-facing) | Can create adversarial culture if punitive |
| Nudge design (defaults, reminders, prompts) | Ongoing | Digital behaviors, data entry, system usage | Requires system access to implement |

## 7. Workforce KPIs

Track these HR/development metrics to measure workforce health and
training effectiveness.

### 7.1 Vacancy Rate
- **Definition**: Percentage of authorized positions currently unfilled.
- **Target**: <8% county-wide; <5% for critical roles.
- **Frequency**: Monthly.
- **Alert**: >12% in any department signals recruitment/retention crisis.

### 7.2 Time to Fill
- **Definition**: Calendar days from position posting to accepted offer.
- **Target**: ≤60 days (≤45 for critical roles).
- **Frequency**: Per hire (reported quarterly).
- **Constraint**: Civil service rules may extend timeline; track separately.

### 7.3 Turnover Rate
- **Definition**: Voluntary separations ÷ average headcount × 100.
- **Target**: <12% annually.
- **Frequency**: Quarterly.
- **Disaggregate**: By department, tenure band (<1yr, 1–5yr, 5–10yr, 10+yr), demographics.

### 7.4 First-Year Turnover Rate
- **Definition**: Employees separating within 12 months of hire ÷ new hires.
- **Target**: <20%.
- **Frequency**: Quarterly.
- **Signal**: High first-year turnover indicates onboarding, job preview, or culture issues.

### 7.5 Training Hours Per Employee
- **Definition**: Total training hours delivered ÷ headcount.
- **Target**: 40 hours/year (ICMA benchmark for progressive jurisdictions).
- **Frequency**: Quarterly.
- **Disaggregate**: By department, role tier, modality.

### 7.6 Training Completion Rate
- **Definition**: Percentage of assigned training completed by deadline.
- **Target**: 95% for mandatory, 70% for elective.
- **Frequency**: Monthly.

### 7.7 Competency Gap Score (Aggregate)
- **Definition**: Average gap score across all assessed competencies within a department.
- **Target**: <1.0 (all competencies within 1 level of target).
- **Frequency**: Annually (post-assessment cycle).

### 7.8 Training-to-KPI Impact Rate
- **Definition**: Percentage of training programs with measurable KPI improvement within 90 days.
- **Target**: 60% of programs show Level 4 (results) impact.
- **Frequency**: Quarterly.
- **Note**: This is the hardest metric to track and the most valuable. Requires discipline in linking training to KPI baselines.

### 7.9 Employee Engagement Score
- **Definition**: Composite score from annual engagement survey.
- **Target**: 4.0/5.0 or top quartile for government employers.
- **Frequency**: Annually.

### 7.10 Internal Promotion Rate
- **Definition**: Percentage of supervisory/management vacancies filled internally.
- **Target**: 60%+.
- **Frequency**: Annually.
- **Signal**: Low rate indicates weak pipeline/succession planning.

## 8. Government Workforce Constraints

When designing KSAB interventions for county government, account for:

- **Civil service rules**: Hiring, promotion, discipline processes are
  governed by merit system rules. Training alone cannot substitute for
  formal performance management.
- **Union agreements**: May define training hours, overtime, shift changes,
  and role boundaries. Cross-training may require negotiation.
- **Budget cycle**: Training budgets are set annually. Multi-year development
  programs need advance planning and council approval.
- **Staff availability**: Front-line staff cannot be pulled for all-day
  training without service impact. Use blended learning and micro-modules.
- **Seniority systems**: Promotion may be seniority-weighted, limiting
  ability to advance highest-performing newer staff.
- **Political transitions**: Leadership changes with elections can disrupt
  long-term workforce strategies. Document and institutionalize programs.

**Framing principle**: Position workforce development as *serving constituents
better*, not as remediation or headcount optimization. Government employees
are public servants — training should honor that identity.

## 9. Succession & Knowledge Transfer

### Risk Assessment Matrix

| Factor | Score 1 (Low Risk) | Score 3 (Medium) | Score 5 (High Risk) |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Retirement eligibility | >10 years | 3–10 years | <3 years |
| Role criticality | Routine, well-documented | Important, some documentation | Mission-critical, tribal knowledge only |
| Replacement difficulty | Common skills, easy to recruit | Moderate specialization | Rare expertise, long ramp-up |
| Knowledge documentation | SOPs current, multiple staff know | Partial documentation | No documentation, one person knows |

**Risk Score** = Sum of four factors (4–20)
- 4–8: Low priority
- 9–14: Medium — begin knowledge transfer planning
- 15–20: Critical — immediate succession action required

### Knowledge Transfer Methods

1. **Documentation sprint**: Subject matter expert creates/updates SOPs
   with a facilitator over 2–3 focused sessions.
2. **Paired assignments**: Junior staff works alongside SME for 3–6 months
   on critical tasks.
3. **Video capture**: Record SME performing complex tasks with narration.
4. **Cross-training rotation**: Rotate backup staff through critical role
   for 2–4 week stints annually.
5. **Decision log**: SME maintains log of judgment calls and rationale
   for 6 months to capture tacit knowledge.

STL County serves ~980,000 residents across diverse communities. Staff must
be equipped to serve all residents equitably, including:
- Geographic diversity (north county, south county, west county, central —
  with significant socioeconomic variation)
- Racial and ethnic diversity
- Age diversity (large senior population using county services)
- Language diversity (Spanish, Bosnian, Vietnamese, and others)
- Disability access requirements

### Equity Competency Development Sequence

| Phase | Focus | Modality | Duration |
|-------|-------|----------|----------|
| 1. Awareness | Understand county demographics, service disparities, implicit bias concepts | eLearning + facilitated discussion | 4 hours |
| 2. Knowledge | Title VI, ADA, language access plan, disparate impact analysis | Classroom + job aids | 4 hours |
| 3. Skills | Use interpreter services, modify communication, analyze disaggregated data | Practice sessions | 4 hours |
| 4. Application | Apply equity lens to own department's KPIs and service delivery | Department workshops | 4 hours |
| 5. Leadership (supervisors+) | Lead equity conversations, address disparities, model inclusive behavior | Coaching cohort | 8 hours |

### Red Flags in Service Equity (Behavioral Indicators)
- Wait times systematically longer at certain locations
- Complaint rates higher for specific demographic groups
- Program participation gaps that mirror socioeconomic patterns
- Staff avoidance of interpreter services when needed
- Inconsistent application of eligibility criteria
